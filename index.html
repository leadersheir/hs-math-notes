Chapter 5 Combinatorics

Permutations

The number of permutations is the number of ways in which \(n\) objects can be arranged taking \(r\) objects at a time, and is denoted by \[ {}^nP_r = \frac{n!}{(n-r)!} \] From which follows \[ {}^nP_n = n! \impliedby 0! \equiv 1 \] The number of permutations of \(n\) objects, where \(k\) groups of similar items have \(a_k\) repetitions, is given by \[ \frac{n!}{\prod_{i=1}^k a_i!} \] For example, if there are three groups with \(a_1\) items in the first group, \(a_2\) items in the second, and \(a_3\) items in the third, then the number of permutations of \(n\) items is given by \[ \frac{n!}{a_1! \cdot a_2! \cdot a_3!} \]
Example
Let's consider the number of arrangements of the word "HIPPOPOTAMONSTROSESQUIPPEDALIOPHOBIA" with all 36 letters taken at once. In our word of interest, h is repeated 2 times; i, 4 times; p, 6 times; o, 6 times; t, 2 times; a, 3 times; s, 2 times; e, 2 times; and the rest (m, n, r, q, u, d, b) are not repeated. So the number of permutations (arrangements) of our word taking all letters at once is \[ \frac{36!}{2! \cdot 4! \cdot 6! \cdot 6! \cdot 2! \cdot 3! \cdot 2! \cdot 2!} \] You can easily evaluate that using a calculator.

Combinations

def Combination. Each of the groups or collections that can be made out of a given number of objects taking some or all of them at a time is called a combination. The number of combinations of \(n\) items taken \(r\) at a time is denoted by \({}^nC_r\). The combination of \(n\) items taken \(1\) at a time is a permutation; taken \(2\) at a time is another permutation; by extension, taken \(r\) at a time is the final permutation. The total number of permutations is obtained by multiplying the total number of combinations of \(n\) items taken \(r\) at a time multiplied by \(r!\). \begin{align} {}^nC_r \times r! &= {}^nP_r = \frac{n!}{(n-r)!} \\ \implies {}^nC_r &= \frac{n!}{r!(n-r)!} \end{align}
Complementarity of Combinations
The complementarity of combinations implies the following \[ {}^nC_r = {}^nC_{n-r} \] This relationship is easily derived from the definition of the number of combinations of \(n\) items taken \(r\) at a time. \[ {}^nC_{n-r} = \frac{n!}{(n-r)!(n-n+r)!} = \frac{n!}{(n-r)!r!} = {}^nC_r \qquad \blacksquare \] The complementarity of combinations follows that \[ {}^nC_x = {}^nC_y \quad \text{and} \quad x \neq y \implies x + y = n \]
The Addition Rule of Combinations
The addition rule of combinations is as follows \[ {}^nC_r + {}^nC_{r-1} = {}^{n+1}C_r \] In general \[ {}^{n+k}C_r = \sum_{i=0}^{k} \begin{pmatrix} k \\ i \end{pmatrix} {}^nC_{r-i} \]
Example \begin{align} {}^{n+2}C_r &= {}^nC_r + 2{}^nC_{r-1} + {}^nC{r-2} \\ {}^{n+3}C_r &= {}^nC_r + 3{}^nC_{r-1} + 3{}^nC_{r-2} + {}^nC_{r-3} \end{align}
Conditional Combination
The number of combinations of \(n\) different things taken \(r\) at a time in which \(p \leq r\) particular things always occur is given by \[ {}^{n-p}C_{r-p} \] The number of combinations of \(n\) different things taken \(r\) at a time in which \(p \leq r\) particular things never occur is given by \[ {}^{n-p}C_r \] The number of combinations of \(n\) different things taken at least one at a time is given by \[ 2^n - 1 \] The number of combinations of any numnber of things taken any number at a time (the total number of things must necessarily be greater than the number of things taken at once) when \(j\) groups of similar items have \(a_j\) repetitions and \(k\) items are unique (not repeated), is given by \[ 2^k \left( \prod_{i=1}^j (a_i + 1) \right) - 1 \] \(k=0\) if there are no unique items. The number of combinations of any number of things taking at least 1 item from each of \(j\) groups of similar items with \(a_j\) repetitions is given by \[ \prod_{i=1}^j \sum_{m=1}^{a_i} {}^{a_i}C_m = \prod_{i=1}^j (2^{a_i} -1) = (2^{a_1} - 1)(2^{a_2} - 1) \dots (2^{a_j} - 1) \] The number of ways in which \((p+q)\) things can be distributed among two specific groups \(A\) and \(B\) such that \(A\) contains \(p\) things and \(B\) contains \(q\) things is given by \[ \frac{2!(p+q)!}{p!q!} \] The number of ways in which \((p+q)\) things can be distributed among two arbitrary groups such that one group contains \(p\) things and the other group contains \(q\) things is given by \[ \frac{(p+q)!}{p!q!} \] The number of ways of distributing \(nq\) things among \(n\) specific groups is given by \[ \frac{(nq)!}{n!(q!)^n} \] The number of ways of distributing \(nq\) things among \(n\) arbitrary groups is given by \[ \frac{(nq)!}{(q!)^n} \]
